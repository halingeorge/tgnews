BBC Scotland’s Darren ‘Loki’ McGarvey insists on the importance of college following Glasgow Clyde College visit

18 Nov 2019, 14:58 by John McNicol

SCOTTISH rapper Darren ‘Loki’ McGarvey has insisted going to college was the key to his success - because he wasn't judged on how he talked or dressed.

Loki, a former Glasgow Clyde College student, went on to win the Orwell prize in 2018 for his acclaimed book Poverty Safari after graduating.

Now the 35-year-old's back at the college to help inspire and mentor current students.

He told the class: “Every success that I am enjoying in my life the now I owe to getting into college.”

Darren, who grew up in Pollok, Glasgow, has gone on to produce BBC Scotland mini-series Darren McGarvey’s Scotland.

In the show, each episode focuses on different issues across Scotland including poverty and crime.

Loki admitted his time at college was difficult – and he wants to help encourage current students to make it through to achieve their qualifications.

Darren, who achieved an HND in Practical Journalism, said: “If it wasn’t for the compassion of the lecturers I probably wouldn’t have made it through.

“Further education is the gateway back in not just to learning but into society.

“I came into college at a time where if I didn’t get on the course – it’s debatable what would have happened.”

And the rapper, who was homeless as a teen, said college is there for everyone – no matter how you talk or dress.

He said: “The course gave me a language to describe all the things I was already doing.

“It taught me how to sound like I could do stuff.

“Cos actually I knew how to deal with people, I knew how to write, I knew how to work with kids cos I was just always trying to be natural.

Every success that I am enjoying in my life the now I owe to getting into college.”Darren McGarvey

“Education is as much them trying to give you a language so that you will be accepted within certain spheres because you speak a certain way or dress a certain way and actually a lot of the stuff you need to know to get on in life.”

Darren believes the college sector should be given more prominence in Scotland’s education system.

He said: “It’s by far the most accessible institution educationally and that extends from everything to the warm welcome you’ll receive whether you are 17 or 49.

“Whether you’re someone with additional support needs, mental health problems, male, female and every other gender you could care to mention.

“The difference between college and university is that FE or HE -whatever you want to call it – a college in itself is more capable of adjusting because unlike universities it’s not rooted in 500 years of institutional rigidity and history and a false sense of its own importance.

“That’s why they need to receive more prominence in the discussion about education.”

Darren, who was 30 when he applied to college, admits that he wouldn’t have committed to a college course in his 20s.

He said: “I left home very, very early I was already bunking about in friends’ houses and lofts when I was 16.

“I was getting looked after by other people’s families for a very long time. I was homeless for three years, I stayed in supported accommodation.

“Now don’t get me wrong, I was still learning.

“I was still active in my community and I was still making music.

“I was still making a name for myself, but the whole concept of committing to a college course was beyond me.”

Darren has openly spoken about his struggles growing up in poverty and his battles with addiction – and admits he still deals with some of these issues today.

Not letting anything get the better of him, he’s now working on his second novel.

He said: “Writing a book is like building a big wall in front of yourself and then one day you just realise you can’t see because you’ve built a big wall in front of yourself.

“And then one day you start breaking though it and then you start realising the book’s nearly done.”

But it’s not always easy.

He admitted: “I’m a nightmare to deal with if you’re a publisher who wants a chapter plan and such and such.

“For me it’s extremely challenging when you’ve got unconventional, shall we say, educational challenges.

“My impulsivity is very high, I don’t regulate well emotionally.

“I focus and obsess on certain things and that’s not contusive sometimes to writing a book where a conventional structure may be required.”

In a final promise to fans, he added: “The book will be out at some point – I don’t know when, but I do think it’ll be a better book than the last one.”
