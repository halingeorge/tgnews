Soybean Harvest Nears Completion While Corn Lags

The Corn Belt’s Northern Edge Still Struggles To Finish.

18 Nov 2019, 21:38 by Dan Looker

DES MOINES, Iowa – As harvest finally winds down, U.S. farmers are nearly finished combining soybeans but remain behind the normal pace on corn, according to USDA’s Weekly Crop Progress Report released Monday.

In the main soybean-producing states, farmers have only 9% left to cut, slightly behind 5% that would remain unharvested on this date, according to Monday’s report. Northern states, hammered by early winter weather, are further behind. Wisconsin has only 77% of beans out of the field, and Michigan was at 76% as of Sunday, the cutoff for the report.

Corn

In its Weekly Crop Progress Report, the USDA pegged the U.S. corn harvest at 76% complete, below a five-year average of 92%.

North Dakota remains the furthest behind, with 23% of that state’s corn harvested, up just a bit from 15% last week and well behind its 85% average for this time of year. Michigan corn is only 39% harvested and Wisconsin is at 44%.

Iowa and Minnesota both lag other states in the heart of the Corn Belt, with each having 77% of their corn harvested. Iowa normally would have 93% of corn harvested. Other state totals include Illinois at 80% vs. a 97% five-year average, and Indiana also at 80% complete vs. a 91% five-year average.

Soybeans

In the major soybean states, the amount of soybeans cut totaled 91%, slightly below a 95% five-year average.

Among Corn Belt states, Nebraska is closest to completion with 99% harvested — also the five-year average. Iowa leads Corn Belt states to the east in soybean cutting, at 95% complete vs. a five-year average of 98%. Illinois is 92% complete vs. a 98% five-year average. Indiana farmers have 91% of their soybeans cut vs. a five-year average of 95%. Ohio is 90% complete vs. a 95% average.
