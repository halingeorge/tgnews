YoungBoy Never Broke Again’s Mother Claims He Kicked Her Out of the House He Bought Her

18 Nov 2019, 22:15 by Trent Fitzgerald

It looks like there's some serious drama going on in YoungBoy Never Broke Again's family.

During an Instagram Live session that surfaced online on Monday (Nov. 18), YoungBoy’s mother, Sherhonda, claimed the rapper kicked her out of the house he bought for her. Apparently, the two had a heated argument, which led to YoungBoy’s mother being thrown out of the home.

“I’m not finna to let you play with me,” she said. “Kentrell (the rapper’s real name) got me a fucking house. He get you house and if you do what he fucking says, he get it took.”

Sherhonda then adds, “I don’t give a fuck, you can have your house. I’ll sleep under the interstate. Bitch I’ll sleep on a muthafuckin' park bench. You will not hold your money over my muthafuckin' head, I don’t give a fuck.”

This scenario feels similar to the moment Blueface kicked his mother and sister out of his house back in June. At the time, Blue kicked his mother, Karlissa, and his sister, Kali, out of his California home while on IG Live. In the video, Blueface and Kali are engaged in a verbal argument while their mother records it. After bickering for a few minutes, Blueface then picks up his mother's clothes and throws them out the door. Afterward, the "Thotiana" rapper called his mother a clout-chaser.

Hopefully, YoungBoy, who just dropped his new project AI Youngboy 2, can work things out his mother.

XXL has reached out to YoungBoy's camp for comment.

These 12 Iconic Hip-Hop Albums Were Nearly Named Differently

6 Feet Deep, Gravediggaz

Original title: Niggamortis

Known as its original title of Niggamortis overseas, Gravediggaz’s debut album got a tamer title for the U.S. consumer.

Cypress Hill, Cypress Hill

Original title: America’s Most Blunted

The original title was more fitting, however, Cypress Hill decided to go the eponymous route.

De La Soul Is Dead, De La Soul

Original title: We Fell Into a Plastic Bottle of Shwingalokate, So We Opened a Radio Station

For the sake of marketing, it’s probably a good idea De La decided to considerably shorten the title of the group's sophomore LP.

It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back, Public Enemy

Original title: Countdown to Armageddon

Recorded under a more apocalyptic name, P.E. eventually decided upon a more militant title for the classic sophomore album.

License to Ill, Beastie Boys

Original title: Don’t Be a Faggot

The Beastie Boys' first iteration of the title for their 1986 debut album was a no-no and ultimately shut down by labels execs.

No Way Out, Puff Daddy & The Family

Original title: Hell Up in Harlem

Puff wanted his Uptown roots to be spelled out on his debut album but opted for something different in the wake of The Notorious B.I.G.’s death.

Only Built 4 Cuban Linx..., Raekwon

Original title: Only Built 4 Cuban Linx Niggaz

Raekwon’s debut album originally had a less market-friendly title, which had to be nixed at the 11th hour.

Reasonable-Doubt, Jay-Z

Original title: Heir to the Throne

There’s no doubt Hov clearly had his eye on the crown when he initially named his debut album Heir to the Throne before switching it up to Reasonable Doubt.

The Marshall Mathers LP, Eminem

Original title: Amsterdam

Following a trip to the Red Light District, Shady considered naming his sophomore major label album after one of the world’s most debaucherous cities.

To Pimp a Butterflly, Kendrick Lamar

Original title: Tu Pimp a Caterpillar

Kendrick Lamar’s sophomore major label album has heavy Tupac influences and was once titled Tu Pimp a Caterpillar, a backronym for Tupac.

Untitled, Nas

Original title: Nigger

The N-word was a little too controversial for Nas’ ninth studio album, so Esco begrudgingly dialed it back, opting for no title.

Views, Drake

Original title: Views From the 6

Drizzy’s fourth solo album originally had a slightly longer name (that nodded to his hometown) but, in the end, he stuck with a one-word title.
