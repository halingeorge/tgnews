Chirlane McCray recalls past depression battle: ‘I just wanted to die’

18 Nov 2019, 22:14 by Julia Marsh

First Lady Chirlane McCray offered a gripping recounting of her own thoughts of suicide — fueled by constant racist bullying she endured as a child — as she kicked off her annual mental health conference Monday.

McCray described to the audience the torment she endured as one of the only black children growing up in Longmeadow, Mass., in the 1960s — her classmates mocked for her skin color, teachers refused to have her in their classrooms, and construction workers spit at her as she rode her bike.

“I didn’t know how to handle all that,” she vividly recalled during her keynote at the Thrive conference at New York Law School Monday.

“I was afraid, I was lonely, I was anxious and so often I just wanted to die,” she said — pausing before continuing her 15-minute address.

“It wasn’t until years later when I started working on my own mental health challenges, anxiety and substance misuse, that I realized my parents probably suffered too from untreated depression,” she said.

It’s the first time McCray has publicly discussed how depression and vicious school-age bullying led her to consider suicide. She has previously discussed her marijuana use.

The first lady received a standing ovation from the group of 140 attendees, including mayors, other elected leaders and behavioral health professionals at the event.

The number attending the opening session of the two-day conference was much smaller than the 224 anticipated by McCray’s staff.

A spokeswoman said she anticipated more people to arrive as the conference continued.

Mayor Bill de Blasio also addressed the gathering Monday afternoon and the pair will greet attendees during an evening reception at Gracie Mansion.

McCray, who launched the city’s $1 billion mental health plan ThriveNYC, also acknowledged its shortcomings.

The program has come under attack by critics for its lack of metrics and for focusing on helping individuals with symptoms like those suffered by McCray.

Just 12 percent of Thrive’s budget helps people with serious mental illness — like schizophrenia.

“We still have an array, a fragmented array, of behavioral health services that let’s too may people feel through the cracks and we still don’t have a unified vision for how to achieve mental health for all,” McCray said in her opening remarks.
