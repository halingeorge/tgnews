Harriet’s Cynthia Erivo admits Joe Alwyn was ‘so gentle’ during tense scenes

18 Nov 2019, 15:26

Cynthia Erivo recalls her experience working with Joe Alwyn on particularly challenging scenes in Harriet, and says he couldn’t have been more ‘gentle’ with her.

The Tony award-winning actress stars as Harriet Tubman in Kasi Lemons’ biopic charting the activist’s escape from slavery in the 1800s, before returning to lead her own family to freedom using the Underground Railroad safehouses.

Joe, 28, stars as slave owner Gideon Brodess which meant having to engage in difficult dialogue with Cynthia while in character. But Joe proved to be a true gentleman and made every effort to ensure the actress was comfortable during their emotionally-charged moments.

Recalling what it was like to work with Joe, Cynthia told Metro.co.uk: ‘He has to be one of the nicest people I have ever met and I know it might sound like I’m just saying it to ease everyone’s mind, but no genuinely, he was the sweetest and he was super worried about me all the time.’

Referring to one particularly harrowing exchange, Cynthia, 32, continued: ‘That very first scene between the tree, he was worried the entire time like, “Are you OK? I don’t want to hurt you. Shall we do it like that? Is this easier for you?” The entire time trying to find out if I was OK, he was so sweet, so gentle, that I knew that I would be safe with him.’

Cynthia admitted she would have felt uncomfortable playing out those ‘twisted’ and ‘abusive’ scenes with an actor who might have ‘relished’ in those moments.

The Color Purple actress added of Joe: ‘There were moments that I would have to calm him down like, “I’m fine. You’re fine because I know that you’re not going to harm me. I know that you’re not going to take advantage. We are OK. Like I had to really like let him know that we have a job to do and we’re going to be OK.

‘He was lovely.’

As Harriet makes her literal journey to freedom in the film, her struggle and eventual triumph of emancipation is brilliantly depicted through her hair and costume.

Cynthia applauds the departments for subtly mastering the art of storytelling through Harriet’s appearance.

‘Paul [Tazewell], from the beginning, used the costume to signify the growth of her and her change, and then her being an enslaved woman into being a woman who was running for her freedom, to being a woman who found her freedom, but hadn’t quite gotten stability,’ she explained.

‘To a woman who is in freedom and stable now and to a woman who’s now worked up enough courage to go back again and now to another woman who becomes a hero.’

She added: ‘It just felt like it was moving so fluidly throughout the film, that I don’t know how we would have done it without him because I truly think that his storytelling helped my storytelling.’

Harriet, which also stars Janelle Monae and Leslie Odom Jr, hits UK cinemas on 22 November.
