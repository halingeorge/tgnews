Brazilian Grand Prix: How a chaotic dead rubber produced F1's best result in years

18 Nov 2019, 10:39 by Luke Slater

Formula One has been a fairly stale and predictable show over the past few years. Mercedes have dominated since 2014, with Ferrari and Red Bull taking the occasional win and gradually chipping away at their dominance.

In the second half of 2019, though, Ferrari and Red Bull have been much stronger challengers to Mercedes. The next generation of F1 stars have gone wheel to wheel with the established greats.

The Brazilian Grand Prix gave us a tough on track battle between two of those; Max Verstappen and Lewis Hamilton, with Mercedes on the back foot and newly-crowned six-time champion Hamilton giving everything he had to challenge the Red Bull. Verstappen, though, was the class of the field.

But there was more to celebrate in a race that produced arguably F1’s best result in years.  It has been nearly seven years since a team outside of Mercedes, Ferrari and Red Bull won a race. So difficult has it been for the other teams to challenge the triopoly that F1’s "Class B" has, rightly, been seriously talked about in the past few years.

A fifth, sixth, or seventh place has often been something to celebrate for teams like McLaren, Alfa Romeo and Toro Rosso. But in Brazil they all had genuine reason to shout.  A chaotic and incident packed race gave us two first time podium finishers and a best-ever fifth for another. These three teams achieved their greatest results in years.

The race itself was pulsating, with divergent strategies at the front, plenty of overtaking, some daring and bold moves and some clumsy and silly ones, too. But the result was even better.

Verstappen vs Hamilton’s captivating duel

12 months ago, Verstappen lost victory and then his head at Interlagos. He collided and spun with Esteban Ocon as the Racing Point attempted to unlap himself, handing a victory to Lewis Hamilton before angrily confronting Ocon in the pit lane. 

This year, though, Verstappen was at his brilliant and faultless best.  Quickest in all three qualifying sessions, he didn't let his advantage slip at the start, bursting off the line to lead Hamilton at the end of the first lap. His afternoon was not without difficulty, though, due to Hamilton's perseverance and two late Safety Car periods that put his win in danger. 

To emerge victorious, Verstappen twice had to overtake Hamilton and he did so with conviction. The first time came after Hamilton jumped the Red Bull at the pit stops but Verstappen brilliantly fought back to reclaim the place within a lap. First, he passed Leclerc, who had held up Hamilton, into turn 12 before coming seemingly from nowhere (albeit with the aid of DRS) to put one down the inside of Hamilton at turn one as the Mercedes jinked across to defend. It was clinical and showed the feel that Verstappen had at Interlagos all weekend. 

The second time was after Red Bull brought him in for a cheap stop and fresh tyres under the first Safety Car period. Mercedes kept Hamilton out, so Verstappen would have to overtake him if he wanted victory. And he did in double quick time.
