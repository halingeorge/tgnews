Torrential rains, budget cuts leave city's back alleys in a rut

18 Nov 2019, 12:00 by Bill Kaufmann

Al Morasch says he and his neighbours have been fighting an ongoing back alley war with trenches.

“About 10 years ago, they dug it down a foot to rebuild it, and they filled the trench with a sand and gravel mix,” said Morasch of the sloping back lane behind his Brentwood home.

“As soon as it rains it just washes out and there’s a big pile of sand and gravel on the street.”

That often leaves a 20 centimetre-deep trench that he and neighbours have filled themselves, he said.

“You could send boats down it,” said Morasch, adding city crews haven’t shown up as often this year.

It’s taken a toll on his neighbours’ vehicles, he added “but I’m fortunate I live at the bottom of the alley.”

That about sums up the past year of discontent over the condition of Calgary’s alleys, after a summer of torrential rains that heavily eroded them combined with a $509,000 cut in the city budget to repair the damage.

That 10 per cent rollback was part of a round of emergency spending cuts last summer to fill in a $60-million funding pothole in the city budget.

It also contributed to a tsunami of 311 complaints about alleys this year — 5,600 compared to 2,463 in 2018.

“That number of complaints is drastic, but those are about alleys that in some cases are impassible,” said Ward 12 Coun. Shane Keating, who sought a city administration study on the issue.

The resulting report also notes the city received 272 millimetres of rain in 2019, well above the 187 mm recorded the previous year.

That foul weather nearly halved the number of days city crews had to repair back lanes because the work can’t be done on wet surfaces, said the report.

“Some lanes had severe damage, so crews had to go back to fix those and couldn’t do regular maintenance,” said Keating.

“And, on top of that, 50 per cent of the work days is a fair amount of time to lose.”

With the city looking to cut more from its budget after the province decided to take an additional $15.5 million in property taxes, alleys could be left even more rutted and potholed, said Keating.

But he said the 10 per cent cut the repair budget has already endured shouldn’t be repeated, particularly when other areas only saw a 2.5 per cent across-the-board reduction.

“Is there stuff we’re doing when we shouldn’t be doing be it, instead of cutting 10 per cent from back lanes?” said Keating.

“Some programs have to be eliminated entirely.”

Calgarians frustrated with the pitted state of their alleys can seek a local improvement provided by a city paving crew if at least 60 per cent of residents approve.

But residents pick up the full tab that can be $6,000 each — a cost paid for over 15 years as an add-on to property taxes.

This year, the city lists 37 such improvements approved this year, but they’re not always a road to satisfaction, said Keating.

“When one of these was done in McKenzie Towne, people used it as a shortcut and were speeding down it,” he said.

He said some of the responsibility has to be borne by motorists who should obey the 15 km/h back lane speed limit to reduce wear on their vehicles.

Michael Shepherd said his Capitol Hill back lane “is more potholes than alley . . . only in the dead of winter are they filled enough to safely drive a vehicle without threat of damage.”

But he expressed resignation over that reality, given both rain erosion and limits to city resources.

“(A city crew) threw some gravel in there, about half-full per hole earlier this year, but the first big rain washed it away,” said Shepherd.

“If cuts are going to be made, residential alleys are an easy go-to,” he said.
