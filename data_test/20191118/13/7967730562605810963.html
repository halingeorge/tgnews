China: One word today covers all bases, hypocrisy

18 Nov 2019, 13:00

To submit a letter to The Age, email letters@theage.com.au. Please include your home address and telephone number.

The juxtaposition of the two foreign interference articles in The Age on Saturday could not have been more galling. The Morrison government's acceptance of China Mengniu's takeover of Bellamy's, even after allegations of market manipulation to lower the price, is in stark contrast to the refusal by Beijing to allow outspoken MPs Andrew Hastie and James Paterson into China for a study tour. The Chinese embassy, as is its habit, added insult by declaring that both men should "repent".

It is not Australia that is actively interfering in other countries' affairs. I teach academic English to students entering into Australian universities, many of whom are Chinese. We have been asked by management not to discuss anything "controversial", such as Hong Kong, the plight of the Uighers, the militarisation of the South China Sea, etc. However, one word that I will be sure to teach this week is "hypocrisy".

Cody Savvidou, Northcote

Are we willing to pay the price?

I am all for a robust stance on human rights within China and freedom for Australian politicians and press to express disdain for Chinese government policy. So far the cost has been denial of visas for two MPs who win kudos for their commentary. We may pay a much greater price in trade and relations and I hope our government and we, as a people, are willing to pay this price. I think it would be wise to count the cost now publicly and, if push comes to shove, identify values more important to us than economic worth. Our moral high ground is somewhat undermined by our government's attitude to other repressive regimes such as Saudi Arabia, to refugees who have arrived by boat and our repression of press freedom. But, sadly, more to the point, our commitment to values may have a price tag we are not prepared to pay and we will be forced by our venality to acquiesce to Chinese government wishes.

Michael Langford, Ivanhoe

There's a mixed message from Canberra

The Coalition government seemingly wants to have it both ways in their "on and off" relationship with China. As demonstrated by the mixed messaging approving the Chinese takeover of Bellamy's while sanctimoniously lecturing its lucrative trading partner about its poor human rights record.

Where there is significant profit to be made (as in the sale of Bellamy's), the government appears only too happy to let a few things slide. Human rights a notable case in point.

Moreover, Senator James Paterson's lament that the "Chinese government is picking and choosing who will be admitted" (into their country just like Australia picks and chooses) among Australia's elected representatives is farcical considering his government's mixed messaging on the subject of Australia-China relations.

So, perhaps our representatives need to go back to the drawing board to unravel their cognitive dissonance (when it comes to China) and to consider our parlous human rights record (when it comes to our First Nation's Peoples and refugees). Just a suggestion.

Jelena Rosic, Mornington

Elders' wisdom

Tony Wright's piece ("Forests blaze, reason goes up in smoke", 16/11), about his family's passing on through the generations of the wisdom and knowledge learned from our traditional owners in relation to land management, wildfire and regeneration – and the despair he shares for how it is mismanaged today – mirrors my own experiences.

Our traditional owners trod lightly on the earth. They didn't use chainsaws or earth-moving equipment to clear the land and degrade the soil structure and canopy cover that are essential in providing sun protection for the survival of forest ecosystems. They nurtured it and were rewarded by it.

The fires across NSW and Queensland are not, as our leaders would have us believe, solely due to prolonged drought. With decade upon decade of deforestation within their native forests for timber supply and agricultural land clearing, vast areas in both states are now baking, and their water catchments have a depleted water yield.

Once you factor in the mismanagement of the Darling River and the exacerbation of climate change, it is time for our political class to now seek out the wisdom of our elders.

Rod Falconer, Alexandra

It's everyone's fault

The recent ill-tempered exchanges between Greens and LNP politicians about climate change in the wake of the recent bushfires are the result of a lack of judgment of voters going back many election cycles. We can't blame any elected official for the fires. We, the voters, are the ones who put them there.

At the last election, people knew that the Liberal and National parties' policies were anti-renewable energy and supportive of fossil fuel industries as opposed to emissions-free technology like wind and solar, yet we still rewarded this disregard for science and lack of foresight with our vote.

Until voters take responsibility and put those candidates and parties who don't advocate for an emergency response to climate change last on our ballot papers, then we will forever be facing this perpetual blame game and still have no proper action to reduce our greenhouse gas emissions.

And we will still wonder when this "perpetual drought" is going to break.
